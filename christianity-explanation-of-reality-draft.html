Christianity Is Not a Religion. It's an Explanation of Reality.

By Billy Broas

The only reason to believe something is because it's true.
That line is often attributed to C.S. Lewis, and while he would sign off on it, the attribution is uncertain.

I'm looking at my philosophy textbook right now. The title is The Pursuit of Truth, and I love that phrase. Truth.

To me it's a North Star. It clears away the clutter and the cobwebs. It may be the most important word in the English language. Vocabulary was never my strongest skill in Latin class, but I remember veritas.

Think of the level at which quantum mechanics operates. The scientists at CERN smash atoms together in Switzerland, discovering the subatomic particles that make up the universe. Christianity operates at that level, and deeper.

The Skeleton

Imagine you're in New York City and you see them building a skyscraper. You see the steel skeleton rising floor by floor. No walls, no windows, no paint. Just the steel structure. And at the top, a pointed steel triangle directed upwards at the sky. Imagine the Empire State Building stripped down.

That image is how I think about reality now, and how I encourage you to look at it. Because if you want to understand the Christian worldview, especially if you, like me, had objections and even hostility to it in the past, this is the actual Christian worldview, which is very rarely the version we're presented with today.

The steel skeleton is the ontological structure. Ontological is a fancy word, but it means what's actually true. It's stripped of emotion. Often, emotion clouds the view of Christianity.

What the structure is tells you what it requires.

The triangular structure at the top of the building is where the whole thing is pointing. It's the goal of Christianity. What the tradition calls sainthood, not a moral achievement, but a transformation of what you are. Full communion with God. I'll explain more of this in future articles, but this is where it's going.

When you participate with reality, when you join the divine dance, you are moving toward the top. Toward God. Toward where human beings are headed.

The moral rules now come into the picture, and they are load-bearing. An engineer reading that steel skeleton can tell you exactly what weight each beam must carry, what the angles require, and what forces are at work.

The ten commandments have the same relationship to reality. Their shape is demanded by the structure, and you can read these moral requirements directly off the ontological fact.

Most people picture Moses coming down from the mountain with the Ten Commandments—don't lie, don't cheat, don't steal—and they assume those rules are either arbitrary, God's personal preferences, or just what works to keep society functioning.

I used to think the same thing.

Before becoming Christian, really Christian, my theory was the social contract. Something our ancestors came up with to keep peace and order. I agreed with all the rules, I just had a different explanation for where they came from. I didn't realize I was making a particular kind of argument, a pragmatic argument, and that it had a fallacy buried in it.

Just because something works doesn't mean it's true.

So the explanation that the Ten Commandments exist because they work? That doesn't hold.

But there's a second problem with the social contract, and it's harder to ignore. The social contract can't answer the hardest question: what about the people who get away with it?

History is full of people who ordered atrocities, lived comfortably, and died with honors. On the social contract view, morality is a human agreement, and the universe has no mechanism for reckoning with what they did. If there's no transcendent standard, if there's no judgment, then the difference between a man who lived rightly and a man who got away with everything is just luck. A morality with no reckoning is a suggestion.

One Truth

But let's go back to that first sentence. Do you see what that assumes?

It assumes that there is an objective reality, a singular truth. And that's actually a good question to ask yourself right now.

Do you believe there is only one truth?

People engage in all these debates about where the universe came from... did it come from the Big Bang, has it always been here, did it come from God... but whatever the answer is, do you believe that there is an answer? And only one answer?

If you do, like I do, and like Christians do, then you believe in objective truth.

I cannot be both myself and my brother at the same time. I cannot be both here and in Sri Lanka at the same time. It cannot be both day and night at the same time. 1 plus 1 cannot equal 3.

This is Aristotle's Law of Non-Contradiction. It's not a religious claim. It's the most basic claim you can make about reality.

And if it's true, then there is a singular truth about where the universe came from, about what we are, and our purpose as human beings.

Now, maybe you don't believe in objective reality. Maybe you believe that multiple contradictory things can all be true at the same time. If so, my writing isn't for you. We can't even have a discussion, because we don't share the most basic premise of any argument.

But if someone believes that, I would also say this: look at their life. There is a zero percent chance they actually live that way. We live as if 1 plus 1 equals 2. We live as if we cannot be in two places at once.

With that in mind, once I understood this idea of objective truth, I had to ask myself: is there an objective morality?

For example, is what the Nazis did objectively wrong, or just something I disagreed with?

Was it just something pragmatically bad because it would lead to other things? If there's no transcendent standard, if there's no unchanging yardstick, then who's to say what they did was actually wrong rather than just something we felt was wrong?

That's an extreme example, but it applies to small daily actions as well.

My wife once wanted me to return something to Target that we hadn't purchased there. Her justification was simple: "You won't get in trouble. They don't care."

I couldn't do it.

(And before you think I'm throwing my wife under the bus, trust me, she's far closer to sainthood than I am.)

I won't argue this Target example on moral or ethical grounds. I'll argue it from the steel skeleton up.

At every scale of reality, from the cell to the relationship to the civilization, you find the same generative pattern: things that give freely to each other and remain distinct tend to flourish. The tradition has a word for that activity: love. Things that extract from each other, or dissolve into each other, tend toward collapse. And what makes the elevator go up, what keeps the whole structure alive, is love active at every floor.

The romantic idea of love is a subset of something much larger. Love, in its full sense, means communion: two entities giving to each other freely while remaining themselves. The kind of giving where both parties come out more fully what they are. This is what generates reality at every level.

Target is an entity. The interaction between a person and that entity is a real relationship, with a real structure. The moral rule about honesty in exchange is not a preference sitting on top of that relationship. It is load-bearing. It describes what the relationship actually requires in order to be what it is.

The Target return is me treating an entity as raw material. I take the relationship and use it for extraction instead of exchange. I treat one of those beams as if it were decorative, as if I can redirect its load without consequence. But the misreading is itself the problem, not whatever might happen downstream. The wrong is in what I'm doing to the structure, not in what the structure might eventually do back.

Once I understood Christianity as this explanation of reality, as a complete view of objective truth, my objections to it collapsed one by one.

So many of my objections, and the objections I see others making, attack straw men, false versions of Christianity.

"But the church is corrupt!"
That doesn't mean Christianity isn't true.

"But believing in God is nothing more than wish fulfillment!"
That doesn't mean Christianity isn't true.

"But suffering exists!"
That doesn't mean Christianity isn't true.

These are all legitimate concerns, and I'll address them eventually, but they have nothing to do with whether or not Christianity is true.

And if the Christian worldview is true, if the Christian God exists, and if he became man in the person of Jesus, then it's the most important event in history. Nothing even comes close.

You see, I have this strange affliction in that I need coherence. I can't silo my beliefs. You know the phrase: Sunday beliefs, Monday actions. I can't live that way.

I was never satisfied being an atheist or agnostic. If you look at my internet browser history in my 20's, you would see loads of visits to Wikipedia pages about humanism, stoicism, and Buddhism. I was always searching.

Christianity is the only one that gives me a complete worldview that accounts for everything. It's the reason why I can't read secular personal development books anymore. It doesn't make sense for me to read anything outside of the coherent system. It's like using a Mac computer and reading a book on how to optimize Windows. It doesn't fit; it doesn't apply.

Now, I have a different end goal.

This version of Christianity that I've learned as an adult, as opposed to the version I learned as a kid, which was, to be honest, intellectually thin, it's the type that critics love to caricature. And fairly so, because it exists.

It's the preacher caught in a scandal. It's the angry man I saw on Hollywood Boulevard pumping a sign, yelling at people that they'll burn in hell. That was my reference point for a long time.

What I didn't know was that behind the caricature sat an intellectual tradition going back two thousand years, one that had already thought through every objection I could raise, and many I couldn't. Finding that tradition changed everything.

The Pattern

The pattern is everywhere. In stories, in architecture, in the shape of the human body, in that skyscraper pointing at the sky. That's what I mean by fractal faith. The same pattern at every scale. From the CERN collider to the Target receipt.

So, that's where I am now.

When I say Christianity is not a religion, I don't mean it has no sacraments, no community, no history. It has all of that. What I mean is that it's not merely a religion, not just a moral code or a tribal identity or a lifestyle choice. It's a claim about the structure of reality itself.

Its real competitors aren't other religions. They're materialism, nihilism, and the idea that the universe just exists for no reason.

The only thing that would falsify my position, that would tell me Christianity isn't true, is either reality itself completely changes, so I wake up tomorrow in Bizarro Land, or another worldview comes along that maps to reality more completely than Christianity does. I don't care about the word. I care about what's true.

So far, nothing has come close.

That's the skyscraper. Steel skeleton. No walls, no paint. Just the structure. And at the top, pointing at the sky.
